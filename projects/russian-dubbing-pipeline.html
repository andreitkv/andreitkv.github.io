The problem

A literal translation does not automatically become believable dubbing.

Translated subtitles often become longer than the original speech. Generated lines then overlap, rush unnaturally or leave awkward gaps, even when every individual tool works correctly.

I built one coordinated pipeline around timing limits. It preserves subtitle structure, shortens overlong Russian phrases, joins broken thoughts and fits each generated voice segment back into the available slot.

Not just translated.
Timed, shaped
and assembled.

Inside the pipeline

Five tools working as one repeatable process.

01

Transcribe

FFmpeg extracts clean mono audio and multilingual Whisper detects the source language automatically.

VIDEO · WAV · SRT
02

Adapt

The Russian text keeps the original timecodes, follows an animation glossary and respects a length budget for every cue.

CONTEXT · GLOSSARY · LIMITS
03

Validate

Invalid subtitle output is rejected. Lines that exceed their slot receive a focused shortening pass instead of restarting the full translation.

SRT CHECK · TARGETED RETRY
04

Fit speech

Voice segments can be sped up, gently slowed or padded. Adjacent fragments are merged when they belong to one sentence.

TTS · TIMING · CACHE
05

Finish audio

Segments are placed on the timeline, mixed, equalised, compressed and normalised before the new audio is attached to the video.

EQ · LUFS · AAC

Key decisions

The automation still leaves room for language and timing judgement.

Meaning before wording

Russian lines are shortened by meaning instead of mechanically cutting characters from a literal translation.

Animation vocabulary

A controlled glossary keeps specialist terms consistent in educational animation material.

Natural speed limits

The pipeline caps time stretching so fitting a slot does not destroy the character of the voice.

Safe reruns

Per-line audio caching and versioned output names make iteration faster without silently replacing a finished result.

Responsible use

The workflow is intended for material I own or have permission to adapt. The resulting Russian track uses a synthetic AI voice and is presented as such.
